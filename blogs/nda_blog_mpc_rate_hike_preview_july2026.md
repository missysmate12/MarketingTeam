# Another Rate Hike Coming? What to Watch Before the 23 July MPC Meeting

Mark the date: **23 July 2026**. That's when the South African Reserve Bank's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) next meets to decide whether interest rates move again. If you're still adjusting to the last increase, the idea of another one landing so soon can feel exhausting.

You don't need to guess in the dark, though. Here's a clear, no-jargon look at what the MPC actually does, what analysts are watching for ahead of this meeting, and — most usefully — what you can do in the days before the announcement if your budget is already under strain.

*(A quick note: this article was prepared ahead of the meeting. Figures on inflation and rate expectations reflect analyst commentary current at time of writing — always check the SARB's own statements for the confirmed, final numbers.)*

---

## What Actually Happens on 23 July

The MPC is a small group within the SARB that meets several times a year to set the **repo rate** — the interest rate at which the Reserve Bank lends to commercial banks. When the repo rate moves, banks' **prime lending rate** moves with it, and prime is what most of your debt — home loans, vehicle finance, credit cards, many personal loans — is priced against.

So when people ask "will interest rates go up in July," what they really mean is: will the MPC raise the repo rate, and will that push prime, and therefore repayments, higher too.

---

## Why Another Hike Is Being Talked About

The MPC's core mandate is keeping inflation — the rate at which prices for everyday things rise — within a target range. Two pressures are feeding the current speculation:

- **Rising oil prices**, which push up the cost of fuel and, in turn, transport and food costs across the economy.
- **Inflation (CPI) readings that have been climbing**, reportedly toward the upper end of what the SARB is comfortable with.

When inflation risk rises, raising the repo rate is one of the main tools the SARB has to try to cool it down. That's why financial commentators are flagging the possibility of a further increase at this meeting — though, as always, nothing is certain until the MPC actually announces its decision. *(NDA will confirm the precise inflation figures and any stated hike probability against the SARB's official communication before this goes live.)*

---

## What a Further Hike Would Mean for You

If the MPC does raise the repo rate again, the pattern will be familiar from May: prime rises by the same margin, and so does the interest portion of anything linked to it.

- **Bond holders** would see their monthly instalment tick up again — the exact rand amount depends on your outstanding balance and remaining term.
- **Vehicle finance** repayments would rise too, typically by a smaller rand amount given shorter loan terms.
- **Credit cards, store cards and personal loans** linked to variable rates would also cost a little more to service each month.

None of these increases exist in isolation. They land on top of whatever pressure your budget is already carrying — fuel, groceries, electricity, school fees. That stacking effect, more than any single line item, is usually what pushes a "tight but coping" household into one that's genuinely struggling.

---

## What to Do *Before* the Announcement — Not After

Waiting for 23 July to see what happens is understandable, but the most useful work happens beforehand.

1. **Know your current numbers.** Write down every debt repayment and its interest rate as things stand today, so you have a clear "before" picture to compare against.
2. **Stress-test your budget.** Ask what happens if your bond, car and card repayments each rise slightly. If that pushes you into the red, that's worth knowing now, not on the 24th.
3. **Avoid new debt in the meantime.** Taking on fresh credit right before a possible rate increase adds risk at exactly the wrong moment.
4. **Build a small buffer if you can.** Even a modest amount set aside now gives you breathing room if repayments rise.
5. **Get ahead of it with a professional assessment.** If your budget is already stretched, you don't need to wait for the outcome to start getting help — a registered debt counsellor can look at where you stand today and how prepared you are for either outcome.
