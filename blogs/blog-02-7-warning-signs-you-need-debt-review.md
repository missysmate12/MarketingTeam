Debt has a way of creeping up on you. One missed payment becomes two. One store account becomes five. Before you know it, you're juggling creditors, dreading your phone, and lying awake at night running numbers that just don't add up.

The National Credit Act defines an over-indebted consumer as someone who cannot satisfy all their financial obligations in a timely manner. But in real life, it doesn't feel like a legal definition — it feels like slow suffocation.

The sooner you act, the better your options. Here are seven signs that debt review may be the right solution for you.

## 1. You're Using Credit to Pay for Everyday Expenses

Groceries. Petrol. Electricity. If you're regularly swiping credit to cover basic living costs, your income is no longer covering your life — debt is. This is one of the most telling early indicators of over-indebtedness, and one of the most important to take seriously.

Debt review restructures your repayments so that you can afford to live again, not just survive on credit.

## 2. You're Paying the Minimum on Multiple Accounts

Minimum payments keep creditors quiet, but they barely touch the capital. When all your accounts are on minimum payment and you still can't make ends meet, you're effectively treading water in an ocean that's getting deeper.

## 3. You've Started Getting Letters and Calls from Creditors

When credit providers begin contacting you formally — through letters, attorneys, or repeated phone calls — it means they've flagged your account as high risk. This is the stage that can lead to legal action and, ultimately, asset repossession.

Under debt review, your creditors are notified within days, and all contact must be directed through your debt counsellor. The calls stop. The letters stop. You breathe.

## 4. You're Scared to Open Your Bank Statements

Financial avoidance is real and very common. If you find yourself deleting SMS notifications, ignoring statements, or feeling a physical sense of dread when your phone rings from an unknown number — that anxiety is telling you something needs to change.

## 5. More Than 40% of Your Income Goes to Debt Repayments

Financial experts generally recommend that no more than 35–40% of your take-home income should go toward debt repayment. If your debt-to-income ratio is higher than this, you're likely over-indebted.

Use NDA's free budget planner on our website to check your own numbers. It takes minutes and could change your financial trajectory entirely.

## 6. You've Missed a Payment in the Last Three Months

One missed payment can begin a domino effect — penalty fees, interest rate hikes, negative credit bureau listings. If you've missed even one repayment recently, now is the time to act proactively rather than wait for the situation to escalate.

## 7. You've Considered Taking Out a New Loan to Pay Off Old Debt

This is the most dangerous warning sign of all. A new loan to pay off existing loans is not a solution — it's a deeper hole. If you've searched for "debt consolidation loans" or approached a lender to cover existing repayments, debt review is almost certainly a better, safer, and more affordable path.

Unlike a consolidation loan, debt review:
- Does not require you to qualify for new credit
- Reduces interest rates (not just restructures them)
- Legally protects your assets from repossession
- Works even if you've been blacklisted

## What Happens If You Ignore the Warning Signs?

Many South Africans wait too long to seek help, often out of embarrassment or hope that things will improve on their own. Sadly, debt rarely resolves itself. Without intervention, the likely outcomes are:

- Summons and court judgments
- Emolument attachment orders (garnishee orders) on your salary
- Repossession of your vehicle or home
- A severely damaged credit record

The good news is that none of this needs to happen. If you act now, debt review can stop the process in its tracks and give you a structured, dignified path to financial freedom.
