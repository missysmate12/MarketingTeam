# Is Your Two-Pot Withdrawal Really Enough? What the Data Says

If you dipped into your two-pot retirement savings this year to deal with debt, you made a decision under pressure — and it was a reasonable one. By early 2026, South Africa's two-pot retirement system had reportedly paid out **R57 billion across 4 million transactions**. You were far from alone in reaching for it.

But if the relief felt smaller than expected, or if the same bills crept back within a few months, you're not imagining it. Reported DebtBusters data shows that many consumers who used two-pot withdrawals to pay down debt still need around **64% of their income** just to service what's left. A lump sum can clear a chunk of debt overnight. It can't always fix why the debt built up in the first place.

This isn't a criticism of the choice you made. It's a look at why a once-off withdrawal and a genuine, lasting fix are two different things — and what to do if the gap between them is still there.

## Why Two-Pot Felt Like the Obvious Answer

The two-pot system was designed to give people limited, controlled access to a portion of their retirement savings without cashing out the whole fund. For someone staring down a call from a credit provider, a shortfall on the rent, or a growing pile of arrears, that access can look like the only lever available — and often, it genuinely is the right one to pull.

Using retirement savings to settle debt isn't reckless. It's a rational response to being squeezed by rising interest rates, load-shedding-related costs, and the everyday expense of living in South Africa right now. There's no shame in having used it.

## Why a Lump Sum Rarely Solves a Structural Problem

Here's the part that catches people out: debt usually isn't a single event. It's a pattern — a monthly gap between income and obligations that built up over months or years. A two-pot withdrawal addresses the *balance* on that debt. It does very little to address the *gap* that created it.

If your income still doesn't comfortably cover your repayments and living costs after the withdrawal, that gap simply reopens. New purchases, one more emergency, or the next rate movement can bring you right back to where you started — except now with less retirement savings behind you.

That's the pattern reflected in the reported 64% figure: even after using two-pot funds toward debt, a large share of income is still tied up in repayments, leaving little room to absorb the next surprise expense.

## An Illustrative (Not Official) Example

To picture this simply, imagine a consumer earning **R15,000 a month** who withdraws **R30,000** from their two-pot savings and uses all of it to pay down credit cards and a personal loan.

**Before the withdrawal (illustrative only):**
- Monthly debt repayments: roughly R9,500 (around 63% of income)
- Left over for living costs: around R5,500

**Immediately after the withdrawal:**
- Some balances are cleared or reduced, so monthly repayments might drop to roughly R7,000
- That feels like progress — for a month or two

**A few months later, without any other change:**
- Rates move, a new expense appears, or repayments creep back up
- Monthly repayments climb back toward R9,000 or more
- The consumer is back to needing the bulk of their income for debt, only now with less retirement savings as a buffer

This is a simplified illustration to show the pattern, not a forecast of any individual's numbers. Everyone's situation is different, which is exactly why a lump-sum withdrawal on its own can't be a complete plan — it needs to be paired with a change to the underlying repayment structure.

## What Actually Changes the Structure

Where a two-pot withdrawal reduces a balance, **debt review** restructures the whole repayment picture. Under the National Credit Act, a registered debt counsellor looks at your real income, your real living costs, and everything you owe — then negotiates new, more affordable terms with your credit providers.

Instead of one lump sum against old debt, you get:
- One consolidated, restructured monthly payment instead of juggling several
- Legal protection for your assets, like your home and car, from repossession while you're under review
- A repayment plan built around what you can actually sustain, not just what's currently owed

Debt review isn't a loan, and it isn't a way to erase debt overnight — outcomes always depend on your individual circumstances, and a debt counsellor will assess your specific numbers honestly before recommending it. What it does offer is something a single withdrawal can't: an ongoing plan that closes the gap between income and obligations, month after month.
