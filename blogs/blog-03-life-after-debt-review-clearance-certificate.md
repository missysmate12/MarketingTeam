The moment you receive your debt review clearance certificate is one worth celebrating. It means you've done something remarkable — you took control of your finances, stuck to a plan, and came out the other side. 

But for many South Africans, this moment also raises a big question: *now what?*

Here's a complete guide to what life looks like after debt review, and how to build a genuinely healthy financial future from this point forward.

## What Is the Clearance Certificate?

Your clearance certificate (also called a Form 19) is the official document issued by your debt counsellor confirming that you have satisfied all the debts included in your debt review — or that you have settled one debt and are no longer over-indebted.

Once issued, your debt counsellor notifies the credit bureaus, and your debt review status is removed from your credit record. This is a pivotal moment. It signals to lenders that you are no longer under debt review and can once again apply for credit.

## Your Credit Score After Debt Review

Let's be honest: your credit score won't be perfect the day after you receive your clearance certificate. Debt review leaves a footprint on your credit record during the process, but once removed, the rebuilding begins.

The good news is that credit bureaus look at your *current* behavior more heavily than your history. You can meaningfully improve your credit score within 12–24 months by following these steps:

**1. Open a basic transactional account**
If you don't already have one, open a standard bank account and use it responsibly. Regular deposits and withdrawals (without going into overdraft) build a positive financial profile.

**2. Apply for a small credit product — carefully**
A low-limit retail account or entry-level credit card, used conservatively and paid in full each month, is one of the fastest ways to demonstrate creditworthiness. Never spend what you cannot pay back immediately.

**3. Pay everything on time**
Payment history is the single biggest factor in your credit score. Not one day late. Set up debit orders where you can.

**4. Keep your credit utilisation low**
Using less than 30% of any credit limit you have signals responsible use to credit bureaus.

**5. Check your credit report regularly**
You're entitled to one free credit bureau report per year from each of the major bureaus in South Africa (TransUnion, Experian, Compuscan, and XDS). Use them. Dispute any inaccuracies.

## Building a Budget That Lasts

One of the most important habits to develop now is budgeting — not as a restriction, but as a superpower. During debt review, you learned what it means to live within a structured financial plan. Don't abandon that discipline now that the pressure is off.

A simple but powerful budgeting approach is the 50/30/20 rule:
- **50%** of take-home income on needs (rent, food, utilities, transport)
- **30%** on wants (entertainment, dining out, subscriptions)
- **20%** on savings and emergency fund

That last category is where many post-debt review South Africans fall short. If you have no savings buffer, any unexpected expense — a car repair, a medical bill — can start the debt cycle again. Aim for at least three months of living expenses saved before you consider any new credit commitments.

## Should You Apply for Credit Again?

There's no simple yes or no answer. It depends on your financial stability, your income, and what you need the credit for.

What we recommend at NDA:
- **Wait at least 6 months** after receiving your clearance certificate before applying for anything significant.
- **Never apply for credit impulsively.** Every application creates an inquiry on your credit record, which can temporarily lower your score.
- **If you're considering a home loan or vehicle finance**, work with a reputable financial advisor first. Make sure your income can comfortably support the new commitment without jeopardising your daily expenses.

## Celebrate the Right Way

You've worked hard. You deserve to acknowledge this milestone. But celebrate wisely — not with a credit-funded splurge, but with something meaningful that fits your budget. A family braai. A short trip. Something that reminds you why financial freedom matters.
