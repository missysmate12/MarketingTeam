# Debt Review vs Debt Consolidation vs Administration vs Sequestration: Which Option Is Right for You?

If you've started searching for a way out of debt, you've probably come across a confusing mix of terms — debt review, debt consolidation, administration, sequestration. They sound similar, salespeople use them loosely, and it's hard to know which one actually fits your situation.

The truth is they are very different processes, with very different consequences for your money, your assets and your credit record. Choosing the wrong one can cost you thousands and leave you worse off than before.

In this guide, we break down each option in plain language — what it is, who it suits, and what to watch out for — so you can make an informed decision and take the right next step.

---

## First, Be Honest About Where You Stand

Before comparing options, it helps to know roughly which group you fall into:

- **You can pay, but it's tight.** Your repayments are manageable for now, but one emergency would tip you over. *Budgeting and small adjustments may be enough.*
- **You can't keep up with your repayments.** Your debit orders bounce, you're borrowing to cover other debt, and creditors are calling. *This is the zone where formal debt relief matters most.*
- **You have no realistic way to ever repay what you owe.** Your debts far exceed anything you could pay off in a reasonable time. *A more drastic legal route may be the only option.*

Keep your group in mind as you read — it points you toward the right tool.

---

## Option 1: Debt Consolidation

**What it is:** Debt consolidation means taking out **one new loan** big enough to pay off several of your existing debts. Instead of five debit orders, you're left with a single monthly repayment to one lender.

**Who it can suit:** People who are still up to date on their payments, have a reasonable credit score, and simply want to simplify their finances or secure a lower interest rate.

**The catch most people miss:**

- It is a **new loan**, not a reduction of your debt. You still owe the full amount — sometimes more, if the repayment term is longer.
- You usually need a **good enough credit record to qualify**, which many over-indebted people no longer have.
- It offers **no legal protection**. If you fall behind on the new loan, creditors can still take legal action and repossess assets.
- It frees up your old credit cards and accounts — which can tempt you to start borrowing again, leaving you with the new loan *and* fresh debt.

Debt consolidation can work for the right person. But for someone already drowning, it often just rearranges the problem instead of solving it.

---

## Option 2: Debt Review (Debt Counselling)

**What it is:** Debt review is a **formal legal process under the National Credit Act (NCA)**. A registered debt counsellor assesses your finances, confirms whether you are over-indebted, and negotiates a **restructured, reduced repayment plan** with all your credit providers.

**How it works in practice:**

- You make **one single, affordable monthly payment** — lower than your combined current repayments.
- That payment is distributed to your creditors on your behalf through a registered payment distribution agency.
- Your **assets — home, car, furniture — are legally protected from repossession** while you're under debt review and keeping to the plan.
- Credit providers **cannot take legal action** against you for debts included in the review.
- It works **even if you are already blacklisted**.

**Who it suits:** People who still have *some* income but can no longer keep up with their full repayments. This is the largest group of struggling South Africans.

**The trade-offs to know:**

- You **cannot take out new credit** while under debt review — which is part of the point: it helps you break the borrowing cycle.
- You'll be flagged as under debt review until your debts are settled, at which point you receive a **clearance certificate** and your record is updated.

Debt review is not a loan and not debt forgiveness. It's a legal shield that gives you breathing room and a clear, structured path back to financial health.

---

## Option 3: Administration

**What it is:** Administration is a court process (under Section 74 of the Magistrates' Court Act) historically used for **smaller debt amounts** — generally where total debt is below R50,000. A court-appointed administrator takes over the management of your debts and distributes payments to creditors.

**Who it can suit:** People with a relatively small total debt who don't qualify for, or don't need, full debt review.

**The catch most people miss:**

- Administrators **charge fees** that are deducted from your payments, which can make the process slow and expensive.
- It can **drag on for many years**, and it places a serious flag on your credit record.
- It offers **less robust protection and oversight** than debt review for most modern over-indebtedness situations.

For most over-indebted South Africans today, debt review is the more comprehensive and better-regulated route — but administration exists for specific smaller-scale cases.

---

## Option 4: Sequestration (Insolvency)

**What it is:** Sequestration is the **legal declaration that you are insolvent** — that your debts exceed your assets and you cannot pay. A court places your estate under the control of a trustee, who **sells your assets** to pay creditors a portion of what they're owed.

**Who it suits:** People in genuinely extreme situations, where there is **no realistic prospect of repaying** the debt and no income to support a debt review plan.

**The serious consequences:**

- You can **lose major assets**, including your home and vehicle, as they may be sold to settle debts.
- It is a **drastic, costly legal process** with long-term effects on your financial standing.
- You remain insolvent until you formally apply for **rehabilitation**, which can take years.

Sequestration is very much a **last resort**. Many people who believe sequestration is their only option are actually better served by debt review — which protects their assets rather than selling them.

## Quick Comparison at a Glance

| Option | Protects your assets? | Reduces monthly payments? | Legal protection from creditors? | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Debt Consolidation** | No | Sometimes (lower rate / longer term) | No | Still up to date, good credit, want simplicity |
| **Debt Review** | **Yes** | **Yes** | **Yes** | Struggling to keep up, but have some income |
| **Administration** | Limited | Yes | Limited | Small total debt (historically under R50,000) |
| **Sequestration** | No (assets sold) | N/A | N/A | No realistic way to ever repay |

## So, Which One Should You Choose?

For most over-indebted South Africans who are still earning *something* but can no longer keep up, **debt review offers the best balance**: it lowers your repayments, protects your home and car, and stops creditors from taking legal action — all under the protection of the National Credit Act.

Debt consolidation may suit someone who is still on top of their payments and simply wants to simplify. Administration serves specific small-debt cases. And sequestration is a genuine last resort for those with no other way out.

The most important thing is this: **the right option depends on your individual numbers** — your income, your total debt, and your assets. A good debt counsellor will tell you honestly which route fits you, even if that route isn't debt review.

## How to Decide With Confidence

Here is a simple checklist to work through before you commit to anything:

1. ✅ List every debt you have, the monthly repayment, and the interest rate
2. ✅ Add up your total monthly repayments and compare them to your take-home income
3. ✅ Be cautious of any "one easy loan" offer that doesn't reduce what you actually owe
4. ✅ Ask whether the option you're considering legally protects your home and car
5. ✅ Speak to a registered debt counsellor for a free, no-obligation assessment
