# Another Rate Hike Coming? What to Watch Before the July 23 MPC Meeting

Mark your calendar: the South African Reserve Bank's (SARB) Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meets again on **23 July 2026**. If that sentence makes your stomach drop a little, you're not alone — the last meeting, on 28 May 2026, raised the repo rate to 7% and pushed the prime lending rate up to 10.5%, and plenty of households are still feeling that increase in their monthly repayments.

So what now? Nobody — not us, not the banks, not the news — can tell you for certain what the MPC will decide on 23 July. What we *can* do is explain, calmly and plainly, what's at stake, and what's worth doing before the announcement lands, especially if your budget is already stretched thin.

## What Is the MPC, Again?

The Monetary Policy Committee is the group at the SARB responsible for setting the repo rate — the interest rate at which the SARB lends to commercial banks. When the repo rate moves, the prime lending rate (what banks charge you) moves with it. That's why an MPC decision made in a boardroom in Pretoria ends up on your bond, car finance or credit card statement.

The MPC meets several times a year and weighs up factors like inflation, the exchange rate and global interest rate trends before deciding whether to hold, raise or cut the repo rate. Analysts and commentators will be watching the usual pressures — things like fuel costs and the inflation outlook — in the lead-up to 23 July, but until the MPC actually meets and announces its decision, any outcome is speculation, not fact.

## If There Is Another Hike, What Would It Mean for You?

We explained the mechanics of the May hike in detail in our [previous article on the rate increase](/debt-advice/repo-rate-hike-2026-what-it-means-for-your-repayments/), so we won't repeat it all here. In short: if the repo rate rises again, prime rises with it, and anything linked to prime — home loans, vehicle finance, credit cards, many personal loans — would cost a little more to service each month.

The size of any increase, if one happens, would depend on the MPC's decision on the day. What matters more for your household budget is the same principle as last time: even a modest increase can be the tipping point if you're already juggling other rising costs, like electricity, fuel or groceries.

## What You Can Do Before the Announcement

Whatever the MPC decides on 23 July, these steps put you in a stronger position either way:

### 1. Know your numbers now
List your current debt repayments and compare them honestly against your take-home income. If you're already close to the edge, don't wait for a possible increase to find out.

### 2. Build in a buffer where you can
If there's any flexibility in your budget this month, consider setting a small amount aside. Even a modest buffer softens the impact of any future repayment increase.

### 3. Avoid new debt in the run-up to the meeting
This isn't the moment to take on new credit "just in case." Extra debt now only adds pressure if repayments do rise later.

### 4. Get ahead of the problem, not behind it
If your repayments are already unaffordable *before* 23 July, that's worth addressing now rather than waiting to see what the MPC decides. A further hike isn't required for a professional assessment to be useful — it's useful the moment the numbers stop adding up.
