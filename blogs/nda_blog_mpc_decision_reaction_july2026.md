# What Happens Next: The SARB's Rate Call and Your Debt

The South African Reserve Bank's Monetary Policy Committee has made its decision for 23 July 2026. Whichever way it went, one thing hasn't changed: if money already feels tight, you deserve a clear, honest answer about what this means for you — not just a headline number.

Below are two versions of this update, one for each possible outcome. You're reading whichever one matches what was actually announced today.

---

## IF THE MPC HIKES

### The SARB Just Raised Rates Again — Here's the Real Cost, in Rand

If you've been anxiously waiting for today's announcement, here's the news: the SARB's Monetary Policy Committee has raised the repo rate by a further 25 basis points, taking it above the 7.00% level set in May. Prime lending — the rate most home loans, vehicle finance and many credit cards and personal loans are priced against — has risen in step.

It's natural to feel a fresh wave of dread reading that. You're not overreacting, and you're not alone — a lot of South African households are doing this same math tonight.

#### What This Actually Costs You Each Month

Basis points can sound abstract, so let's make it concrete. As a rule of thumb, a 25-basis-point increase adds roughly **R168 a month for every R1 million** you owe on a 20-year home loan. So if you owe R1 million on your bond, expect somewhere in that region extra each month on the home loan alone. Owe R2 million, and it's roughly double that. Vehicle finance, credit cards and personal loans linked to prime will also cost a little more to service, generally by a smaller rand amount because those terms are shorter.

On its own, that number might feel manageable. The problem is that it rarely arrives on its own. This increase lands on top of:

- The May 2026 hike, which many households are still adjusting to
- Municipal electricity tariffs that rose again from 1 July 2026
- Fuel and transport costs that have stayed elevated for months
- Whatever grocery and everyday cost pressure you were already carrying

It's the stacking of these costs — not any single one — that turns a tight-but-coping budget into one that simply stops balancing.

#### Why the SARB Made This Call

The MPC's job is to manage inflation, not to make life harder for households on purpose. When inflation risk sits above the level it's comfortable with, raising the repo rate is one of the few direct tools it has to try to cool it down over time. That's genuinely cold comfort when you're the one absorbing the extra cost this month — which is exactly why having a plan matters more than understanding the theory.

#### What to Do About It This Week

You can't undo today's decision, but you have more control over your own budget than it might feel like right now.

1. **Recalculate your real numbers.** List every repayment at its new amount alongside your essential costs. It's uncomfortable, but it turns a vague sense of dread into facts you can work with.
2. **Prioritise your most expensive debt.** Credit cards, store accounts and personal loans usually carry the highest interest — any spare room in your budget does the most good there.
3. **Talk to your creditors before you miss a payment, not after.** Lenders generally prefer a conversation to a chase, and communicating early keeps you in a stronger position.
4. **Don't borrow your way through it.** New credit to cover old repayments tends to make next month's numbers worse, not better.
5. **If the maths genuinely doesn't work anymore, get a professional view.** That's not a failure on your part — it's the sensible next step when your income and your repayments have stopped adding up.

#### When a Shrinking Budget Needs More Than Budgeting

If today's increase is the one that tips your budget from stretched to unworkable, that's precisely the situation debt review exists for. It's a legal process under the National Credit Act: a registered debt counsellor looks at your full financial picture and negotiates a single, restructured monthly payment with your credit providers — while your assets stay protected from repossession and creditors can't take legal action against you for included debts. It isn't a loan, and it doesn't erase what you owe, but it does mean the next MPC announcement doesn't have to feel like a threat, because your repayments are already set at a level your income can sustain.

## IF THE MPC HOLDS

### Rates Stayed the Same Today — Here's Why That's Relief, Not Resolution

If you were bracing for another increase, here's some genuinely good news: the SARB's Monetary Policy Committee has decided to hold the repo rate steady, leaving it at the level set back in May. Your bond, vehicle finance and other prime-linked repayments aren't going up because of today's announcement.

Take a moment to feel relieved about that — it's a real, if modest, bit of breathing room, and it's fair to be pleased with it.

#### But Here's the Part Worth Being Honest About

A hold means things haven't got *worse* from here. It doesn't mean the pressure that's already built up has gone away. Since the May hike, households have also been absorbing:

- Higher bond, vehicle and credit repayments from that increase, which are still with you every month
- Municipal electricity tariff increases from 1 July 2026
- Ongoing fuel and transport costs that have stayed elevated
- The everyday creep of grocery and living costs

None of that reverses today. A hold simply means no *new* rate-driven cost has been added this month — the cumulative squeeze from everything before it is still exactly where it was yesterday. If your budget was already uncomfortably tight going into this announcement, it's still uncomfortably tight coming out of it.

#### Why the SARB Chose to Hold

Economists were genuinely split heading into today's meeting — some expected another hike given inflation running above the SARB's comfort level, others expected a hold given how much households have already absorbed since May. A hold suggests the Committee judged that the recent increase, plus other cost pressures already in the system, are doing enough work for now without adding more. It's a reasonable, defensible call — but it's about managing inflation nationally, not about whether your specific household budget is coping.

#### What to Do With This Breathing Room

A hold is a good moment to use the pause productively, not to relax completely.

1. **Use any relief deliberately, not by default.** If a hold frees up even a little compared to what you'd budgeted for a hike, redirect it toward your most expensive debt rather than letting it quietly disappear into day-to-day spending.
2. **Revisit your numbers as they stand today**, not as they were before May. Confirm your current repayments against your actual income and essential costs — the picture may have shifted more than you realise.
3. **Don't take today's hold as a green light for new credit.** The underlying cost pressures haven't reversed, so this isn't the moment to take on more.
4. **If you were already stretched before today, that hasn't changed either.** A hold doesn't fix an unaffordable budget — it just means it didn't get harder today.
5. **If the last few months have already pushed you past your limit, get an honest assessment now** rather than waiting for the next announcement to decide for you.

#### If the Pressure Hasn't Actually Lifted

For many households, a hold is welcome news that still doesn't change the fact that the budget isn't working. If that's you, debt review is worth a look regardless of what the repo rate does next. It's a legal process under the National Credit Act: a registered debt counsellor assesses your full financial situation and negotiates one restructured, more affordable monthly payment with your credit providers, with your assets protected from repossession while you're in the process. It isn't a loan and it won't erase your debt, but it does mean your repayments stop being at the mercy of the next MPC decision, whichever way that one goes.
