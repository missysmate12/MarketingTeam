# Is Your Two-Pot Withdrawal Really Enough? What the Data Says

If you dipped into your two-pot retirement savings this year to pay down debt, you're in very good company. Since the system opened up access to a portion of retirement savings, South Africans have withdrawn a significant amount of money between them — reportedly in the region of R57 billion across around 4 million withdrawals by early 2026 — and a large share of that money went straight towards settling debt.

If that was you, please don't feel any guilt about it. Using money you had legal access to, to relieve real pressure on your household, was a sensible decision made with the information and options available to you at the time. This article isn't here to second-guess that choice.

What it is here to do is answer the question a lot of people are quietly asking themselves a few months later: **"I took the money out and used it on my debt — so why doesn't it feel like anything has changed?"**

Let's unpack what the data suggests, and what you can do next.

## A Quick Recap: What Is the Two-Pot System?

Since the two-pot retirement system came into effect, a portion of your retirement fund contributions is split into a "savings pot" (which you can access before retirement, once a year) and a "retirement pot" (which stays locked away until you retire). The savings pot withdrawal is taxed as income in the year you take it, and once you've withdrawn from it, you need to wait for the next allowed withdrawal window to access more.

In other words: it's a **once-off, limited pool of money**. It was never designed to be an ongoing income stream — it's a single lump sum you can tap for a specific need.

## Why the Relief Doesn't Always Last

Here's where the numbers tell an important story. Industry debt data — most notably from DebtBusters — suggests that many of the same consumers who withdrew two-pot savings to pay down debt are still needing a very large share of their income, reportedly around 64%, just to service their remaining debt repayments each month.

Read that again: even after a lump-sum payment towards debt, a large chunk of take-home pay is still going straight back out the door to creditors.

Why does this happen? A few common reasons:

- **The withdrawal reduced a balance, but not the monthly obligation.** Paying R30,000 off a credit card can feel like progress, but if your minimum payments, interest rates and number of accounts stay the same, your monthly squeeze barely changes.
- **It's a once-off event, and debt is an ongoing pattern.** If the underlying gap between income and expenses hasn't changed, new debt (or the same old habits) can creep back in within months.
- **Tax reduces the amount you actually receive.** Because the withdrawal is taxed as income, what lands in your account is meaningfully less than the headline number — sometimes not enough to make a real dent across multiple accounts.
- **It doesn't restructure your interest rates or terms.** A withdrawal pays down principal at today's terms; it doesn't renegotiate anything.

## A Simple Before-and-After Illustration

To see the difference between a one-time withdrawal and structural debt restructuring, consider a simplified example of someone juggling a credit card, a store account and a personal loan:

**Before any action:**
- Total monthly minimum repayments across three accounts: high, and spread across different due dates and interest rates
- A two-pot withdrawal is used to make a lump-sum payment towards the credit card

**After the withdrawal:**
- The credit card balance is lower, but the minimum monthly payment is only slightly reduced
- The store account and personal loan repayments are completely unchanged
- Total monthly outgo on debt is still high relative to income — because two of the three accounts were never touched

**After debt review, by comparison:**
- All qualifying debts are consolidated into a single, restructured monthly instalment
- A registered debt counsellor negotiates the terms with each credit provider
- The monthly repayment is brought into line with what the household can actually afford
- Assets like a home or car are legally protected from repossession while the process runs

The withdrawal treats one symptom, once. Debt review is designed to address the structure of the debt itself, on an ongoing basis.

## So Was Using Two-Pot a Mistake?

No — and it's worth saying that plainly. For many households, that withdrawal was the only accessible source of relief at a moment of real financial strain, whether that meant catching up on arrears, avoiding legal action, or simply getting through a difficult month. It bought breathing room, and breathing room matters.

The issue isn't the decision to withdraw. It's the assumption — an easy one to make — that a single withdrawal was meant to be a *complete* solution. The data suggests that, for a lot of people, it wasn't. And that's useful to know now, rather than finding out the hard way when the next tight month arrives.

## What You Can Do From Here

If your two-pot withdrawal helped but didn't fix things, here's a practical next step:

1. **List every debt you currently have**, including balances, interest rates and monthly minimums — updated to reflect any two-pot payments already made.
2. **Compare your total monthly debt repayments to your take-home income.** If debt repayments are consuming a large share of your income, that's a sign the underlying structure — not just the balances — needs attention.
3. **Avoid using a future two-pot withdrawal as your only plan.** It can be part of a strategy, but it shouldn't be the whole strategy.
4. **Get a professional view of your full picture.** A registered debt counsellor can look at everything — not just one account — and tell you honestly what your options are.
