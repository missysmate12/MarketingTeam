Misinformation is one of the biggest barriers between struggling South Africans and the financial relief they deserve. Every day, people who desperately need debt review talk themselves out of it because of something they heard from a colleague, read on social media, or assumed was true.

Let's set the record straight on the six most persistent myths about debt review in South Africa.

---

## Myth 1: "Debt Review Will Ruin My Credit Score Permanently"

**The truth:** Debt review is a temporary status, not a permanent black mark.

While you are under debt review, a flag is placed on your credit record to show that you are in an active debt restructuring process. This means you cannot take on new credit during this time — which is actually a protection, not a punishment.

Once you have completed debt review and received your clearance certificate (Form 19), the debt review flag is removed from your credit record entirely. From that point, your credit rebuilding journey begins, and with responsible financial behaviour, many South Africans see meaningful improvements in their credit scores within 12 to 24 months.

---

## Myth 2: "I'll Lose My House and My Car"

**The truth:** Debt review is designed to *protect* your assets — not strip them from you.

One of the core purposes of debt review under the National Credit Act is to shield your home, vehicle, and other assets from repossession while you repay your debts through a restructured plan. As long as you maintain your debt review repayments, your credit providers cannot legally repossess your assets or take legal action against you.

In fact, debt review is often the only way to stop an imminent repossession. If you're already behind on your bond or vehicle finance, contacting a registered debt counsellor immediately could be what saves your most important possessions.

---

## Myth 3: "Only People Who Are Completely Broke Need Debt Review"

**The truth:** Many debt review clients are employed, earning decent salaries — they're just over-committed.

South Africa's credit culture encourages easy access to credit — clothing accounts, store cards, personal loans, vehicle finance, home loans — and many working South Africans find themselves servicing six, seven, or eight different accounts every month. The problem isn't necessarily income; it's the ratio of debt obligations to that income.

A teacher, a civil servant, or a retail manager earning a stable salary can absolutely be over-indebted. Debt review is for anyone whose monthly debt repayments leave them unable to cover basic living expenses — regardless of income level.

---

## Myth 4: "My Employer Will Find Out and It Will Affect My Job"

**The truth:** Debt review is a confidential process and does not need to be disclosed to your employer in most circumstances.

Your debt counsellor communicates with your credit providers, not your employer. The exception would be if a garnishee order (emolument attachment order) has already been placed on your salary by a creditor *before* you entered debt review — in which case debt review can actually help you work toward having that order reviewed.

Entering debt review is a legally protected, private financial decision. There is no requirement to inform your employer.

---

## Myth 5: "Debt Review Takes Forever — I'll Be Stuck for Decades"

**The truth:** Most NDA clients complete debt review within 36 to 60 months.

The duration depends on the total amount of debt, the restructured interest rates, and the revised monthly repayment amount. Debt counsellors negotiate reduced interest rates with your credit providers, which means more of every payment goes toward reducing the actual capital — accelerating the process.

You can also make additional lump-sum payments at any time to settle accounts early. Some clients finish their debt review well ahead of schedule.

---

## Myth 6: "I Can Just Ignore My Debt and It'll Eventually Disappear"

**The truth:** Unpaid debt in South Africa does not simply disappear. And the longer you wait, the worse it gets.

While prescription of debt (under the Prescription Act) can, in certain narrow circumstances, cause some debts to expire after three years of no acknowledgement or payment, this is not a reliable or recommended strategy. Credit providers actively monitor and pursue unpaid accounts. The more common outcomes of ignoring debt are:

- Summons and default judgments
- Emolument attachment orders (garnishee orders) deducted directly from your salary
- Blacklisting at credit bureaus
- Asset repossession by the sheriff

These consequences are far more disruptive and financially damaging than the structured, manageable process of debt review.
