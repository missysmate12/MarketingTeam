## BLOG POST

# Your July Bills Just Got More Expensive — Here's How to Cope

If your money feels tighter this month than it did a few months ago, it's not your imagination and it's not poor planning on your part. Three separate cost increases have landed on South African households within about 60 days of each other. On their own, any one of them would be manageable. Together, they're squeezing budgets that were already stretched thin.

Let's break down exactly what's changed, what it could mean in rand terms for a typical household, and — most importantly — what you can do about it this week.

---

## Three Cost Shocks, Two Months

**1. Electricity: up 9.01%, effective 1 July 2026**

Municipal electricity tariffs increased by 9.01% from 1 July 2026. If you're on a municipal account (rather than buying directly from Eskom), this is the increase you'll now see reflected in your monthly bill or prepaid units.

**2. Interest rates: prime now at 10.5%**

On 28 May 2026, the South African Reserve Bank (SARB) raised the repo rate to 7% — the first hike since 2023 — which lifted the prime lending rate to 10.5%. If you have a bond, vehicle finance, or credit linked to prime, your minimum repayments went up from that date.

**3. Petrol: a record R28.06 a litre**

As of 3 June 2026, petrol reached a record R28.06 per litre. Transport costs overall are now running at roughly 9.4% higher than a year ago. Whether you drive to work, rely on taxis, or just notice it in the price of everything that gets delivered by road, fuel is quietly taking a bigger bite out of every rand you spend.

Individually, each of these is a news headline. Stacked within two months of each other, they're a real test of any household budget.

---

## What This Could Look Like for a Typical Household

Every household's numbers are different, so treat the figures below as a **simple, illustrative example** — not an exact calculation of your own situation.

Imagine a household with a R1 million home loan, an average municipal electricity bill of around R1,500 a month, and a car that uses about 60 litres of petrol a week.

- **Electricity:** A 9.01% increase on a R1,500 bill works out to roughly **R135 more a month**.
- **Bond repayment:** Following the May rate hike, a R1 million bond at the new prime-linked rate costs in the region of **R168 more a month** than it did before the hike (based on the standard 20-year home loan maths covered in our May 2026 rate hike article).
- **Petrol:** At R28.06 a litre, 60 litres a week comes to around **R1,684 a week**, or roughly **R7,290 a month** — before you even count taxi fares, deliveries, or the cost of getting kids to school.

Add the electricity and bond increases together and you're looking at roughly **R300 extra a month** disappearing before you've bought a single extra loaf of bread — on top of a fuel bill that, at today's record price, is already one of the biggest line items in many households' budgets.

For a household with little to no slack, an extra R300 a month plus a heavier fuel bill can be the difference between a budget that just balances and one that doesn't.

---

## Why This Is Happening (In Plain Language)

It helps to understand *why*, even if it doesn't make the numbers any smaller.

- **Municipalities** raise electricity tariffs annually, largely to cover the rising cost of buying bulk electricity and maintaining ageing infrastructure.
- **The SARB** raises the repo rate to help manage inflation — the idea is that slightly more expensive credit today helps keep the cost of everyday goods from spiralling further tomorrow.
- **Petrol prices** are driven mainly by international oil prices and the rand's exchange rate, both of which are largely outside any single household's control.

None of these decisions were made *at* you personally. But they land on your bank account all the same, and understanding the "why" doesn't pay the bill — a plan does.

---

## What You Can Do About It This Week

You can't change tariffs, interest rates, or oil prices. But you can control how you respond to them.

**1. Update your budget with the real numbers.** Don't guess — pull your latest municipal bill, check your new bond or car repayment, and calculate your actual weekly fuel spend at current prices. An accurate budget is uncomfortable but far more useful than a hopeful one.

**2. Look for genuine, sustainable cuts first.** Geyser timers, trip-planning to cut unnecessary driving, and cancelling unused subscriptions won't undo three cost shocks at once, but every rand saved reduces the gap.

**3. Don't fill the gap with more credit.** It's tempting to put petrol or groceries on a credit card "just this month." That relief is real but short-lived, and it adds another repayment to next month's already tighter budget.

**4. Add up all your monthly debt repayments against your take-home pay.** If your total debt repayments — including the new, higher bond or car instalment — are eating up more than roughly 40% of what you take home, it's worth getting a professional view on whether your current arrangement is still sustainable.

**5. Get an independent assessment before things slip further.** The earlier you act, the more options are available to you.
