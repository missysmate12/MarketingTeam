# Your July Bills Just Got More Expensive — Here's How to Cope

If it feels like everything got more expensive at once, you're not imagining it. In the space of about six weeks, South African households have been hit with three separate cost increases: interest rates went up, petrol hit a record price, and now, from 1 July 2026, municipal electricity tariffs have risen by 9.01%.

On their own, any one of these would be manageable. Together, they're squeezing budgets that were already stretched thin. Here's what's actually changed, what it's likely costing you, and — most importantly — what to do if the numbers no longer add up.

---

## The Three Shocks, Explained Simply

**1. Electricity — up 9.01% from 1 July 2026**
Municipal electricity tariffs increased by just over 9% this month. For an average household, that's a real, unavoidable increase to a bill you can't simply stop paying.

**2. Interest rates — prime now at 10.5%**
The SARB's May 2026 repo rate hike pushed the prime lending rate up, meaning bigger monthly repayments on bonds, vehicle finance, credit cards and many personal loans.

**3. Petrol — a record R28.06 a litre**
Record fuel prices in June 2026 don't just cost you at the pump. They ripple through to the price of groceries, transport and just about everything that gets delivered by road — which is almost everything.

Individually, each of these is a headline. Together, they're a squeeze from three directions at once, all landing within about two months of each other.

---

## What This Actually Costs a Typical Household

Numbers make this real. Take a household with a R1 million home loan, a car, and a weekly commute:

- The electricity increase alone could add somewhere in the region of R150–R300 to a typical monthly bill, depending on usage.
- The May rate hike adds roughly R168 a month for every R1 million owed on a 20-year bond — so around R168 extra just on the home loan, before accounting for vehicle finance or credit cards linked to prime.
- At R28.06 a litre, a household doing 60 litres a week is spending noticeably more on fuel than it was even a few months ago — and that's before the flow-through effect on food and transport costs.

Stack those together, and a household can easily be facing several hundred extra rand in unavoidable monthly costs — without having changed a single spending habit.

---

## Why the Combination Matters More Than Any Single Increase

A single price increase is something most budgets can absorb, eventually. The real danger is *compounding* — when several increases land close together, before a household has had time to adjust to the last one.

This is exactly the kind of moment that turns a "tight but manageable" budget into one that no longer balances. If you're feeling that squeeze, please know: this isn't a sign you've mismanaged your money. It's a sign that external costs have risen faster than most households can realistically absorb in such a short space of time.

---

## A Practical Checklist for This Week

1. **Recalculate your electricity budget** using your latest bill and the 9.01% increase.
2. **Check your bond and vehicle finance statements** against the new prime rate of 10.5% — the increase may already be reflected.
3. **Track your actual petrol spend** for one week at current prices, rather than relying on last month's number.
4. **List every debt repayment** you have, in one place, with up-to-date amounts.
5. **Add it all up.** If your total monthly debt repayments are creeping past 40% of your take-home pay, alongside these new living costs, it's worth getting a professional view before things get harder.

## When the Numbers Stop Working

If you've done the maths above and the total simply doesn't fit anymore, you have options — and none of them start with panic.

**Debt review**, a legal process regulated under South Africa's National Credit Act, allows a registered debt counsellor to assess your full financial picture and negotiate a single, more affordable monthly repayment with your credit providers. While you're under debt review, your assets are legally protected from repossession, and credit providers can't take further legal action against you for included debts.

Debt review isn't a loan, and it isn't a way to erase what you owe. It's a structured way to bring your repayments back in line with what you can actually afford — which, with three cost increases landing at once, is exactly the kind of recalibration many households need right now.
