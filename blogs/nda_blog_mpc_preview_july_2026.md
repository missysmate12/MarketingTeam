# Another Rate Hike Coming? What to Watch Before the July 23 MPC Meeting

Mark the date: the South African Reserve Bank's Monetary Policy Committee (MPC) meets again on **23 July 2026**. For anyone who felt the last increase — the repo rate rose to 7.00% on 28 May 2026, taking prime lending to 10.5% — this next announcement is understandably nerve-wracking.

We don't know what the MPC will decide. No one outside that room does. But here's a calm, plain-language guide to what the meeting is about, what's feeding into the decision, and — most usefully — what you can do *before* the announcement, regardless of which way it goes.

---

## A Quick Refresher: What the MPC Actually Does

The MPC is the SARB committee responsible for setting the repo rate — the rate at which the Reserve Bank lends to commercial banks. That rate flows through to the prime lending rate, which is what most South African home loans, vehicle finance, and many credit cards and personal loans are priced against. When the repo rate moves, your repayments generally move with it, whichever direction it goes.

The committee's main mandate is managing inflation. When it sees pressure that could push prices up too quickly, raising rates is one of the tools it has to try to cool things down. If you'd like the fuller explanation of how this mechanism works and what May's increase cost households, we've covered that in detail in our earlier piece on the May 2026 rate hike.

---

## What's Likely Feeding Into This Meeting

A few pressures have built up since the last meeting that the MPC will be weighing:

- **Fuel costs.** Petrol hit a record R28.06 a litre from 3 June 2026, partly driven by Middle East conflict pushing Brent crude above $100 a barrel. Fuel prices feed directly into inflation, both at the pump and through transport costs across the economy.
- **Electricity tariffs.** Municipal electricity tariffs rose by an average of 9.01% from 1 July 2026, on top of the increase municipalities absorbed from Eskom in April — another direct cost pressure on households and a factor in the broader inflation picture.
- **The starting point.** May's hike to 7.00% was already the first increase since 2023, ending a period many households had hoped signalled further rate relief instead.

None of this guarantees a particular outcome on 23 July. It simply explains why another hike is a real possibility that's worth preparing for rather than dismissing.

---

## What to Do Before the Announcement — Whichever Way It Goes

**Know your exposure now.** Check whether your home loan, vehicle finance, credit card or personal loan is linked to prime. If it is, you already know roughly how a further 25-basis-point move would affect your monthly repayment — a useful number to have in your back pocket either way.

**Build in a buffer where you can.** If there's any room at all in your budget this month, using it to get ahead on your most expensive debt (usually credit cards or store accounts) gives you a bit more cushion if repayments do rise again.

**Don't wait for the announcement to act if you're already stretched.** If your budget is only just balancing *before* 23 July, a further increase could be the point where it stops working altogether. It's far better to get a professional view now than to wait and react afterwards.

**Avoid new debt in the meantime.** Taking on additional credit while there's uncertainty about rates is rarely a good idea — it adds risk exactly when you'd want less of it.

---

## If Your Budget Is Already Under Pressure

If the May increase already pushed you close to your limit, you don't need to wait for 23 July to find out whether things get harder. Debt review, a legal process under the National Credit Act, allows a registered debt counsellor to assess your full financial picture and negotiate a single, restructured monthly payment with your credit providers — protecting your assets from repossession while you regain stable ground.

It's not a loan, and it doesn't erase what you owe. But it does mean you're not left hoping the next MPC decision goes your way — your repayments are set at a level your income can actually sustain.
