**Facts verified before drafting (see sources below the checklist):**
- Debt review is a statutory process under the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (as amended by the National Credit Amendment Act 19 of 2014), applied for under Section 86, run by a registered debt counsellor.
- Applying for debt review does **not** require a credit check or a minimum credit score — it is an affordability assessment of the consumer, not a credit application, which is why it remains available to consumers who are already blacklisted or in default.
- Once an application for debt review is lodged, credit providers are barred from taking further legal action for as long as the consumer keeps to the process/plan — but timing matters: debts on which a *default judgment has already been granted* generally cannot be included in the restructured plan, so acting before judgment is entered gives stronger protection than acting after.
- Credit bureaux place a "under debt review" flag on the consumer's profile — this is explicitly not the same as a negative/blacklist listing, but it does mean no new credit can be taken while under debt review.
- A debt consolidation loan is an ordinary new credit agreement. Under Section 81 of the NCA, any registered credit provider must run an affordability assessment before granting it, and in practice this means a credit check and a reasonably clean repayment history — mainstream consolidation lenders generally decline applicants who are already in arrears. Some "bad credit" consolidation products exist but are not guaranteed and are typically costlier.
- A consolidation loan carries no built-in statutory protection — it doesn't pause or stop legal action on other debts, and if the application is declined (common once someone is already behind), it leaves the reader no further along.
- Framed qualitatively rather than with any invented percentage or statistic, per compliance guidance — no unverifiable numbers are used anywhere in this piece.

# Debt Consolidation Loan or Debt Review? How to Choose When You're Already Behind

If you're juggling several repayments and searching for a way out, you've probably come across two options that keep popping up: taking out a **debt consolidation loan**, or going onto **debt review**. They get talked about as if they're two versions of the same thing. They're not — and knowing the difference could save you months of frustration.

There's no need to feel embarrassed about being unsure which one applies to you. Most people aren't taught how either option actually works until they need one. This guide walks through both, plainly, so you can work out which path fits your situation — or find out you might already know the answer.

## The Core Difference, in One Sentence

A **debt consolidation loan** is a brand-new loan — you still need to qualify for it, at a rate based on your credit profile, and it doesn't touch any legal action already in motion. **Debt review** is a regulated legal process under the National Credit Act (NCA) that restructures what you already owe into one affordable payment, with no credit check required to apply, and with legal protection built in.

In short: one is *new credit*. The other is a *legal process for managing existing credit*. That single distinction explains almost every other difference between them.

## Debt Consolidation Loan vs Debt Review: Side by Side

| | **Debt Consolidation Loan** | **Debt Review** |
|---|---|---|
| **What it is** | A new loan used to pay off several existing debts, leaving you with one new repayment | A regulated legal process where a debt counsellor restructures your existing debts into one affordable monthly plan |
| **Credit check required?** | Yes — lenders must run an affordability and credit assessment before approving it | No credit check to apply — it's an assessment of your income and expenses, not your credit score |
| **Do you need a decent credit record?** | Generally yes — most mainstream lenders decline applicants who are already in arrears | No — it's designed for consumers who are already struggling, in default, or blacklisted |
| **Stops legal action already under way?** | No — it doesn't pause or halt any existing legal process on its own | Yes, in most cases — once you apply, credit providers are generally barred from further legal action while you keep to the process, though debts with an existing default judgment usually can't be included |
| **Effect on your credit record** | A new credit agreement appears on your profile like any other loan | Credit bureaux add a "debt review" flag — not the same as a blacklisting, but it does mean no new credit while you're under review |
| **Regulatory oversight** | Provided by an NCR-registered credit provider, but otherwise a private commercial loan agreement | Run by a registered debt counsellor under the NCA, with oversight from the National Credit Regulator and, where needed, the courts or National Consumer Tribunal |
| **Interest rate** | A new rate, set by the lender based on your risk profile — can be higher if your credit record is already under strain | No new loan or new interest rate is issued; your debt counsellor negotiates with your existing credit providers on your behalf |
| **Who it tends to suit** | Consumers who are current on payments, have a reasonably healthy credit record, and would still qualify for a decent rate | Consumers who are already behind, blacklisted, facing legal action, or who wouldn't qualify for a consolidation loan on affordable terms anyway |

## Why This Confuses So Many People

Both options are trying to solve the same visible problem — too many repayments, not enough breathing room — so it's easy to assume they're interchangeable. They're not, because they sit on completely different legal footing.

A consolidation loan is, at its core, a bet that a lender will approve you for one bigger loan at a workable rate, and that doing so will genuinely leave you better off each month rather than simply moving the debt around. That bet depends entirely on your current credit standing. If you're already missing payments, most registered lenders will see the same risk you're trying to escape — which is exactly why affordability assessments exist under the NCA in the first place.

Debt review isn't a loan at all. Nobody lends you anything. A registered debt counsellor looks at everything you owe and everything you earn, and works out one restructured monthly payment that fits your actual income — with your legal protection as a built-in feature of the process, not something you have to separately negotiate.

## A Simple Decision Checklist

Ask yourself these questions, in order:

1. **Are you current on all your repayments, or only slightly behind?**
   If yes, a consolidation loan may genuinely be worth exploring — but only if it would lower your total monthly outgoings, not just move debt from one account to another.

2. **Would you likely be approved for a consolidation loan at a fair rate today?**
   If you're not sure, or you suspect your credit record wouldn't hold up to a lender's affordability check, that uncertainty is itself useful information.

3. **Are you already blacklisted, in default on one or more accounts, or have you received a letter of demand or summons?**
   If yes, debt review is very likely the safer path — and if there's any legal action already in motion, timing matters, so it's worth getting advice quickly rather than waiting.

4. **Do you just want one lower payment, or do you need actual legal protection while you catch up?**
   A consolidation loan can simplify your payments. Only debt review gives you the legal shield that comes with the NCA process.

If you answered "not sure" to more than one of these, that's not a failure — it's simply a sign that a professional assessment, rather than guesswork, is the right next move.

## Neither Option Is a Magic Fix

It's worth saying plainly: neither a consolidation loan nor debt review makes debt disappear, and no responsible advisor should ever promise you a guaranteed outcome or a fixed reduction before actually assessing your finances. Both are tools. The right one depends on where you're standing right now — not on which one sounds more appealing.
