# Your Repayments Just Went Up: A Calm Guide to the May 2026 Rate Hike

If you've noticed your bond or car repayment creeping higher, you're not imagining it — and you're certainly not alone. On 28 May 2026, the South African Reserve Bank (SARB) raised the repo rate to 7%. It was the first increase in three years, and it nudged the prime lending rate up to 10.5%.

For a lot of South Africans who had been quietly hoping for rate *cuts*, that news landed hard. After years of stretched budgets, even a small increase can feel like the last thing you needed.

Take a breath. This article explains, in plain language, what actually changed, how it affects the money leaving your account each month, and — most importantly — what you can do about it. No jargon, no judgement. Just a clear picture and a practical way forward.

---

## First, What Are the Repo Rate and the Prime Rate?

These two terms get thrown around a lot, so let's keep it simple.

- The **repo rate** is the interest rate the SARB charges when it lends money to banks. It's the country's "base" interest rate, and the SARB adjusts it to help manage inflation — the rising cost of everyday things like food, fuel and electricity.
- The **prime lending rate** is the rate banks charge their customers, and it moves in step with the repo rate. When the repo rate goes up, prime goes up too.

Most of your debt — your home loan, vehicle finance, credit card and many personal loans — is linked to prime. So when prime rises from one level to the next, the interest portion of those repayments rises with it.

In short: **a higher repo rate usually means higher monthly repayments** on the debt you already have.

---

## What This Actually Costs You Each Month

Here's where it gets real. The size of the increase depends on how much you owe and over how long.

To give one widely reported example: on a 20-year home loan, the May 2026 hike adds roughly **R168 a month for every R1 million** you owe. So a household with a R2 million bond is looking at somewhere in the region of a few hundred rand more each month — on the home loan alone.

Car finance is affected too, though usually by a smaller rand amount because the loan term is shorter. Credit cards, store accounts and personal loans linked to prime will also cost a little more to service.

On their own, these increases might sound manageable. The problem is that they rarely come on their own. They stack on top of:

- Rising electricity tariffs
- The ongoing cost of fuel and transport
- Food prices that keep climbing
- Whatever debt pressure you were already carrying

It's the *combination* that catches people out. One extra increase can be the straw that tips a tight-but-coping budget into a budget that no longer balances.

---

## Why Did the Reserve Bank Raise Rates Now?

It's natural to feel frustrated — it can seem like the deck is stacked against ordinary households. But the decision wasn't made to punish anyone.

The SARB's main job is to keep inflation under control. When it sees risks that prices could rise too quickly, one of its few tools is to raise the repo rate. Higher rates encourage saving and slow down borrowing and spending, which helps cool inflation over time.

The idea is that protecting the value of your money over the long run is worth a bit of short-term pain. That's genuinely cold comfort when you're the one feeling the pain in your monthly budget — which is exactly why having a plan matters more than understanding the theory.

---

## The Warning Signs You've Crossed From "Stretched" to "Over-Indebted"

A rate hike doesn't just cost you a few extra rand. For some households, it quietly pushes them over a line — from *managing* their debt to being *over-indebted*. Over-indebtedness simply means your required debt repayments have become more than you can realistically afford alongside your living costs.

It's worth being honest with yourself here, without any shame. You may have crossed that line if:

- You're using your credit card or a new loan to cover existing repayments or month-end essentials
- You're juggling which debts to pay each month, letting some slide
- You're paying mostly interest and fees, with the actual balances barely moving
- You feel a knot of dread when your phone rings or a debit-order date approaches
- There's simply nothing left — and now even less — once the debit orders have gone off

If you recognised yourself in two or more of those, please don't panic, and please don't ignore it either. These are signals, not verdicts. The earlier you act, the more options you have.

---

## What You Can Do Today

You can't control the repo rate. But you have more control over your own situation than it might feel like right now. Here's a practical place to start.

### 1. Recalculate your real numbers

Sit down and list every debt repayment, the new amounts where they've gone up, and your essential monthly costs. Seeing it written down is uncomfortable, but it replaces a vague sense of dread with facts you can actually work with.

### 2. Tackle the most expensive debt first

Unsecured debt — credit cards, store accounts and personal loans — usually carries the highest interest. If you have any room at all, channelling extra towards the most expensive debt slows the damage. Even small, consistent amounts help.

### 3. Speak to your creditors early — not late

If a specific repayment has become unaffordable, contact that credit provider *before* you miss a payment. Some may offer short-term arrangements. It's never guaranteed, but lenders generally prefer to talk than to chase. Communicating early keeps you in a stronger position.

### 4. Resist the urge to borrow your way out

Taking new credit to cover old credit feels like relief for a week or two, then makes everything worse. A higher-rate environment is exactly the wrong time to add more debt to the pile.

### 5. Check whether debt review is right for you

If the maths simply doesn't work anymore — if your repayments have outgrown your income — **debt review** is a legal process under the National Credit Act designed for precisely this situation. We explain it below.

## How Debt Review Can Help in a Higher-Rate Environment

When repayments rise and a budget stops balancing, debt review is one of the strongest forms of protection available to South African consumers under the National Credit Act (NCA).

Here's how it works:

- A registered debt counsellor assesses your full financial situation — your income, your living costs and all your debts.
- They negotiate a **restructured repayment plan** with your credit providers on your behalf.
- Instead of several repayments, you make **one single, more affordable monthly payment**.
- While you're under debt review, your assets — like your home and car — are **legally protected from repossession**, and credit providers can't take legal action against you for the debts included in the process.

A few important, honest points:

- Debt review is **not a loan** and it is **not a way to wipe out your debt**. It's a structured, legal way to make your existing debt manageable while you get back on stable ground.
- Outcomes depend on your individual circumstances — a debt counsellor will look at your specific numbers and tell you honestly whether it's the right fit.
- It does mean you can't take on new credit while you're in the process, which is part of how it helps you recover.

For many people, the real value is simpler than any of the technicalities: it turns an overwhelming pile of demands into one plan they can actually live with.

Interest rates rise and fall — that's the nature of the economy, and no one can promise where they'll go next. What you *can* do is make sure that the next change, in whichever direction, doesn't catch you unprepared.

If the May 2026 increase has tightened things to breaking point, that's not a personal failure. It's a sign that it's worth getting a clear, professional look at your options — sooner rather than later.
