## BLOG POST

# Why South Africa's Highest Earners Are Drowning in Debt

If you picture someone who needs debt counselling, you're probably not picturing a professional earning a healthy salary, driving a decent car, and living in a good suburb. That's exactly the assumption the latest data challenges — and it's worth sitting with for a moment, whichever side of it you're on.

According to DebtBusters' Q1 2026 Debt Index, South Africans earning **R50,000 or more a month** now carry a **303% debt-to-income ratio**, and are spending **101% of their take-home pay on debt servicing** each month. In plain terms: as a group, this is people who are spending more on debt repayments alone than they actually earn.

That's not a typo, and it's not a low-income statistic. It's reported data on some of the country's highest earners — and it says something important: **being financially stressed and being "successful" are not opposites.**

## The Assumption This Data Quietly Breaks

There's a common — and understandable — belief that debt review is a service for people who are struggling to make ends meet on a modest income. It's easy to see why: most conversations about debt, budgeting and financial stress in South Africa focus on the rising cost of living for everyday households.

But debt doesn't check your payslip before it becomes unmanageable. A bigger income usually comes with bigger financial commitments to match — a larger bond, newer vehicle finance, private school fees, a credit card with a higher limit "because you can afford it." Each of those decisions can be entirely reasonable on its own. Stacked together, and financed at today's interest rates, they can quietly add up to a monthly debt load that no longer fits, even on a good salary.

If the DebtBusters figures reflect anything, it's that this isn't a hypothetical risk — it appears to already be the lived reality for a meaningful slice of South Africa's higher-income earners.

## How "Doing Well" Becomes "Financially Stretched"

Nobody plans to end up spending more than they earn on debt. It tends to happen gradually, through a pattern often called **lifestyle inflation** — where spending rises in step with income, leaving little or no extra breathing room even as earnings grow.

A few common contributors worth recognising, without judgement:

- **The bond that matched a promotion.** A bigger home loan taken on when income rose, sized for the salary at the time rather than for changing interest rates or family circumstances later.
- **Car finance as a status marker.** Vehicle repayments that made sense against one income but become heavy once other commitments (school fees, a second car, home renovations) are added on top.
- **Credit cards used as a buffer, not a tool.** A high limit intended for convenience or emergencies that becomes a rolling balance carried month to month.
- **Personal loans that consolidate stress, not debt.** Taking a loan to cover a shortfall this month, which then becomes another repayment competing with everything else next month.
- **School and education costs.** Fees that are a genuine priority for many families, but which — combined with everything above — leave very little room to absorb an interest rate increase or unexpected expense.

None of these choices are careless. They're the ordinary financial decisions of people building a stable life. The trouble is that "stable on paper" and "sustainable in practice" aren't always the same thing — and interest rate increases, like the one South Africa saw earlier in 2026, can expose the gap between them quickly.

## Why Pride Keeps High Earners Quiet

Perhaps the biggest barrier isn't the maths — it's the mindset. Many professionals and high earners feel they're simply not "supposed" to need help with debt. There can be a quiet fear of judgement: from colleagues, from family, from themselves.

If that sounds familiar, please hear this clearly: **feeling financially stretched has nothing to do with how hard you work, how smart you are, or how much you earn.** The DebtBusters data suggests you would be in good company among people earning exactly what you do. Recognising a problem early — while you still have income and options — is not a failure. It's the single most useful thing you can do for your future self.

## Debt Review Isn't Only for "Rock Bottom"

There's a persistent myth that debt counselling is a last resort reserved for people who've missed payments or lost their income entirely. In reality, debt review — a formal process regulated under South Africa's National Credit Act — exists for anyone whose debt repayments no longer fit comfortably alongside their living costs, regardless of income bracket.

For higher earners, that can mean:

- A registered debt counsellor reviews your full financial picture — income, essential costs and every credit agreement — confidentially and without judgement.
- Where appropriate, your repayments can be restructured into a single, more manageable monthly instalment negotiated with your credit providers.
- While under debt review, your assets are legally protected from repossession for the debts included in the process.

It's worth being upfront: debt review is not a way to erase debt, and it isn't automatically the right fit for every situation — a qualified debt counsellor will assess your specific numbers honestly before recommending it. But for someone earning a strong salary and still feeling the monthly squeeze, it can be the structured reset that turns "spending more than I earn on debt" into a plan that actually works.

## What You Can Do This Week

You don't need to wait for a crisis to take stock. A few practical starting points:

1. **Add up every debt repayment** — bond, vehicle finance, credit cards, personal loans, store accounts — and compare the total honestly against your take-home pay.
2. **Look at the trend, not just the snapshot.** Has your debt load grown faster than your income over the last two or three years?
3. **Be honest about "invisible" stress signals** — using credit to cover credit, dreading month-end despite a decent salary, or feeling that a single emergency would tip things over.
4. **Get a professional, confidential view** before deciding whether restructuring makes sense for you.
