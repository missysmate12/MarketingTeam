You don't need a financial expert to tell you that 2026 is tough. Grocery bills are up. Petrol is expensive. Eskom tariff hikes have pushed electricity costs higher. And interest rates, while showing some relief, continue to weigh heavily on households with variable-rate debt.

If you're feeling the squeeze, you're not alone. And you're not helpless.

Here is a practical, honest guide to managing your household finances in one of the most economically challenging periods South Africa has seen in recent years.

---

## Step 1: Know Your Numbers (Even If They're Scary)

The most powerful thing you can do right now is sit down and write out exactly what comes in and what goes out every month. No approximating. No rounding.

**Income:** All sources — salaries, child support, rental income, side hustle earnings.

**Fixed expenses:** Rent or bond, vehicle finance, school fees, insurance premiums.

**Variable expenses:** Groceries, fuel, electricity, data, entertainment.

**Debt repayments:** List every single account — the creditor, the balance, the monthly instalment, and the interest rate.

This is your financial baseline. Until you see it clearly, you cannot improve it. Use NDA's free budget planner on our website if you'd like a structured template to guide you.

## Step 2: Cut With a Scalpel, Not an Axe

When people try to budget in a crisis, they often go too aggressive too fast — cutting everything — and then give up within two weeks because it's unsustainable. Small, consistent changes are more powerful than dramatic ones that don't last.

**Groceries:**
- Plan meals for the week *before* you shop, not after.
- Shop with a written list and stick to it. Supermarkets are designed to make you spend more.
- Buy store-brand (no-name) products for staples like flour, oil, rice, and cleaning products. The quality difference is often minimal, but the savings are real.
- Batch cook on Sundays. One chicken can become three meals.
- Check the day-old bread section. It's perfectly good and significantly cheaper.

**Electricity:**
- Geyser timers are one of the highest-impact investments you can make. Your geyser is one of the biggest electricity consumers in your home. A timer ensures it only heats water when you need it.
- Switch lights off when you leave a room — it sounds basic, but it adds up.
- If you can afford it, a small solar kit can dramatically reduce your Eskom dependence over time.

**Transport:**
- Plan your errands in a single trip rather than multiple outings.
- If you have a vehicle, explore carpooling with colleagues. Even alternating weeks saves meaningful amounts on fuel.
- Check whether your vehicle's tyre pressure is correct — underinflated tyres increase fuel consumption.

**Subscriptions and services:**
- Audit every debit order on your bank statement. You may be paying for streaming services, gym memberships, or apps you've forgotten about.
- Consider whether you need multiple streaming platforms. Rotating subscriptions — subscribing to one for a month, cancelling, trying another — can be a cost-effective strategy.

## Step 4: Build Even a Small Emergency Fund

It sounds counterintuitive when money is tight, but a small emergency fund is what prevents manageable financial strain from becoming a crisis.

Start with a goal of R1,000. Put R50 or R100 away each month in a separate savings account (not the account your debit orders come from). Once you reach R1,000, aim for R2,500. Then R5,000.

The goal over time is three months of living expenses, but don't let the end goal paralyse you from starting. Every rand in that account is a rand that stops you from needing to swipe a credit card when your car battery dies or your child gets sick.

## Step 5: Protect Your Income

In a tough economy, your income is your most important financial asset. Think proactively about how to protect and grow it.

- **Upskill:** Free and affordable online courses (Coursera, Udemy, YouTube) can position you for better opportunities.
- **Side income:** Even R500–R1,000 per month from tutoring, baking, car washing, or selling pre-loved items can make a meaningful difference.
- **Review your tax:** Many South Africans are entitled to tax deductions they never claim — medical expenses, home office use, retirement annuity contributions. Speak to a tax practitioner.
- **Know your rights at work:** If you're facing retrenchment, understand your Section 189 rights under the Labour Relations Act (the process your employer must follow) and your minimum severance entitlement under the Basic Conditions of Employment Act.
